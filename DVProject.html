Musk to lead Twitter after closing the Largest Buyout in History

After announcing the biggest buyout in history, Elon Musk, CEO of Tesla and SpaceX, is set to become Twitter’s interim CEO once the $44 billion takeover deal is closed, according to a Reuters report.

Musk, the world’s richest man, was revealed to be Twitter’s largest shareholder in an SEC filing on April 4th with a 9.2 per cent stake. A day later the social media giant named Musk to its board to avoid the possibility of a takeover, capping his stake at 14.9 per cent. However, on April 10th, Musk reversed his plans and Parag Agarwal, CEO of Twitter announced the same in a tweet.

“Elon’s appointment to the board was to become officially effective 4/9, but Elon shared that same morning that he will no longer be joining the board. I believe this is for the best,” said Agarwal, who took over as CEO from Jack Dorsey last year. “We have and always will value input from our shareholders whether they are on Board or not.”

Shares of Tesla have plunged 16.5 per cent since April 6th on the speculations that Musk’s involvement with Twitter will divert him from running the world’s largest electric car company. The sale of $8.5 billion worth of Tesla stock by Musk ahead of the $44 billion cash transaction and the lining up of a margin loan of $12.5 billion against his Tesla shares also added to the decline.

Twitter shares, on the other hand, have gained about 4 per cent in the last one week in response to the 28 per cent premium offered by Musk over Twitter’s stock before Musk’s initial investment was announced.

“He’s paying a very high price,” said Lawry Daniel, technology analyst at finnCap Group. “He has thrown his hat in the ring. It will be interesting to see what he does with the social media business.”

Earlier this week, Musk declared a number of ultra-high net worth investors who will be providing over $7 billion in funding for his Twitter bid, including Oracle’s co-founder Larry Ellison, Sequoia Capital, Binance and Fidelity.

Binance CEO, Changpen Zhao, who has committed $500 million to Musk-Twitter deal told Financial Times that his crypto exchange would offer unconditional support for the takeover of Twitter.

“It’s more of a blank cheque,” said Zhao. “We hope to be able to play a role in bringing social media and web3 together and broadening the use and adoption of crypto and blockchain technology.”

Other investors include Brookfields Asset Management, tech-investment groups Vy Capital and DFJ Growth Partners and Qatar’s sovereign wealth fund, QIA.

According to reports, in case Musk fails to close the deal with Twitter, he would have to pay $1 billion in termination fee and also risk being sued by the social media company.

Cineworld continues to be the most shorted stock



British cinema company Cineworld continues to be the most shorted stock on the London Stock Exchange for eighth month in a row despite the company enjoying box office success for multiple blockbusters since December 2021.

The short interest stands at over 8 per cent for the world’s second largest cinema chain which recently lost a case against Canadian rival Cineplex, the latter seeking $900 million in damages over cancelation of a takeover deal.

The damages amount to more than Cineworld’s market capitalization and has raised doubts over the company’s viability.

The business also has significant debt with net borrowings at $5.04 billion as of December’21 which overshadows its positive net cash flow of $25 million.

The rise in the popularity of streaming services during the pandemic has also raised concerns as production houses have started releasing movies directly on OTT platforms with subdued demand seen for in-cinema viewing.

Mooky Greidinger, CEO of Cineworld however, remains upbeat about the business’s future prospects.

“We are in good shape moving forward. If there are no big covid surprises, I hope we are turning the direction of the ship,” Greidinger said. “To the analysts, I do not agree with everything that they write. I fully respect what they are saying but this is their job and my job is to run the company.”

Shares of Cineworld have declined 35 per cent year-to-date.

Online fashion retailer, Boohoo, is the second most shorted stock in FTSE with 6.8 per cent of its outstanding shares borrowed to sell. The cost-of-living crisis in the UK has slowed the revenue growth of the company and increased its operating costs.
